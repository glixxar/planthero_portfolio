Project: PlantHero

PlantHero is an interactive tool that makes it simple and easy to find the perfect waterwise plant for you! Waterwise plants are sturdy, resilient plants that require only minimal watering to survive. By planting waterwise plants, you are helping to fight the Australian drought crisis and can also reduce your water bill at the same time.



My role in this project was coding for the website. This involved creating the main html pages for each criteria of the website and then adding the relevant css to these pages to match the design which was agreed on by the team's lead designer.

Project Development

Over the course of the unit, our project went through several iterations because of added and scrapped functionalities based on user feedback.

Part A: Design Concept Proposal and Pitch

Product Requirements

  • Design a webtool called ‘PlantHero’ that Australian amateur gardeners can use to find the perfect waterwise plant to suit their living space or garden, without having to do any research.
  • Filter user input against a waterwise plants database obtained from the Queensland Government

Contributions

  • Creation of relevant user personas to cater the website to.
  • Front-end UI and back-end design, creating low-fidelity testing plan.

Project Part A Reflection

Part A went well as we managed to hash out the vision we have for our project. At the start, we had some worries as to how we were going to implement a 2D garden for users to use to plan their garden. Eventually, the idea evolved into creating a set of prompts which gradually narrows down the options for users until they have 10 plants to choose from. These points were later improved on in the latter part of the unit with the addition and removal of some prompts based on feedback from users.

Archetypal Users

With our users being amateur gardeners, users will utilise PlantHero to learn which waterwise plants are suitable to be planted at their desired location. Most of the users will be relatively new to gardening and will be seeking a tool which mitigates the requirement to do in depth plant-suitability research prior to purchasing a plant.

Building upon the target audience analysis above, I along with input from our project manager, developed three personas representing who I feel will be the archetypal users of PlantHero.

Initial front-end design

Initially, the webpage was designed as seen above with the planthero logo along with two buttons representing the find my button progressive prompt and find by name prompt. At this stage, the buttons had minimal styling without any hover effects or tooltips to indicate users have their mouse cursor over the button. The other pages also follow a very similar design style with different shades of green being used.

Part B: Creating the Minimum Viable Product (MVP)

MVP Progress

  • The implementation of the find my plant function had been nearly completed. The series of prompts which are included are the select your future plant's location, select the type of soil, select the amount of sunlight, select the size of your plant and the select your colour preferences. The website will then match these selections against the waterwise-plants dataset to provide users a small list of plants to choose from no greater than 10.
  • The ‘Search by name’ function was slightly appended to include the ability to search by a variety of plant descriptors (such as type and colour). The page is essentially an input with a button which users can type in various attributes and names of plants to provide users with plant recommendations from the dataset.

Contributions

  • Creating html pages for each prompt in the find my plant function and styling them with the appropriate css.
  • Implementing an input form and its accompanying buttons for the search by description function.
  • Adding tooltip hover effects for buttons to guide users between webpages.

Project Part B Reflection

Through our user-testing, we managed to refine our MVP with the removal of the initial select plant attributes prompt as new amateur gardening users would be overwhelmed by the amount options they have as most of these attributes would be known to only experienced gardeners. During our demo, we had also managed to present our fully completed find my plant function to receive feedback from our peers. With this, we continued to work on improving the accuracy and functionality of the find my plant function and build on the find by description function. Together as a team we also decided to remove the 'I'm feeling lucky' function as it did not serve a distinct function on the website.

Html Pages for the Find My Plant function

As seen in the images, the completed prompts for the find my plant function were made with a minimalistic design with a light green background to emphasize the gardening aspect of our website. The prompts are also match the interaction plan that was drafted by our lead designer Vanesssa. A simple transform effect that makes buttons bigger was added to give visual cues to our users when navigating our website.

Input form for Search by Description

This image shows the design of the search by description function which was partially implemented during the Part B demo and fully implemented by the Part C tradeshow. The user can type in any keywords such as 'tree', 'rose' or 'cactus' or even the plant's name to bring up a list of results from the waterwise database.

Implementation of tooltip hover effects

After several sessions of user testing, our project manager Cameron proposed to the group that we should implement tooltips to guide our users through our website. Following this, I added several useful hover effects which tell them what clicking a particular button would do such as skipping a page or going back and adding the website description to the logo on the main homepage.

Project Part C Reflection

Overall, I felt that our website was successful as all our visitors managed to navigate our website perfectly without any errors. There were some changes to the find by name and find my plant function as it user testing determined that some prompts were unnecessary and the find by name function was modified to be search by description to make the search range more broad. Although these changes were necessary, it was disappointing to not be able to implement the original functionalities that were decided on during Part A.

Feedback from users on UI and Interactivity

Prior to finishing the final product, we created a list of criterion for success which, if met, would mean we could call the project a success. This was done through user testing of the final product on 7 individuals who provided us with feedback which we were able to document. The results of this was discussed in the report.

Overall Course Reflection

Meeting Expectations

Thinking back to my first journal entry, I feel that my original expectations were met up to a certain degree. Firstly, the expectation that I would be working on a website along with others was fulfilled as together as a team we worked to create a website from scratch with different people working on different aspects of the website such as design, coding and documenting. Coming together towards the end to put each person's piece of the puzzle together to produce a working and functioning website as stated in our MVP was a great experience. However, there were several difficulties which accompanied this process.

The course was limited from the start due to the covid-19 pandemic preventing student to student contact and relegating students and tutors to just using discord as a means of conducting classes and student interaction. As a result of this, communication was difficult and was fraught with countless issues as internet problems, microphone issues, student shyness and discord technical problems were abundant. Furthermore, discord was not inherently meant to be used for education purposes. Sometimes it was just a difficult experience to listen to tutors in a channel with a hundred other students of which some forget to mute their mic or just have very bad feedback issues.

Adding to this, as the course was originally designed to be conducted face to face, several of the activities were awkward as some of the ice breakers were difficult to carry out without looking at a person's facial expressions instead relying only on their voices. In general, it was better to allow students to form their teams at an earlier stage as being able to interact with our team members earlier meant that students would be able to achieve rapport and bond faster.

Over the duration of the course I felt that working together with others carries its own set of difficulties as opposed to working alone as different people have different commitments and priorities. As a result, getting everyone to submit their part of the project was hard as their priorities may be different to mine. What I learned from this was that we must be patient towards others and be firm and understanding when trying to push others to finish their part of their assignment. A simple message with an offer for help would go a long way in reminding group members that their part is due.

Learning across the course

Now that the course has ended, I feel like there were a few things I managed to learn from the course.

The workshop sessions helped me to learn on how to apply and use php to supplement html code. The contact sessions also helped me to learn how to structure and organise when I write my report. Furthermore, I also learned the intricacies of how to build a website for users using user testing instead of building a website for developers.

Recommendations

Allow students to form teams faster and of their own choosing as less time would be wasted on activities with students who we would not interact with after team forming.

Encourage students to pay better attention during contact sessions to ensure students are clearer on course materials.

Incorporate more individual work throughout each project part to provide students more control of their marks.

Provide sample websites for students to understand the website quality expectation.
